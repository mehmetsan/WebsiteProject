Orphan Drugs


The commonly named 'orphan drugs' are designed to cure illnesses so rare that sponsors are hesitant to produce them under conventional marketing conditions. The path from the discovery of a new molecule to its commercialization is long (10 years on average), costly (several tens of millions of euros), and very unpredictable (among ten molecules tested, only one may have a therapeutic effect). The development of a drug intended to treat a rare disease does not allow the capital invested in its research to be recovered.

Orphan medicinal products may be defined as:

Drugs that are not produced by the pharmaceutical industry for economic purposes but that lead to the need for public health. In fact, indications of a drug may also be considered 'orphan' as a substance may be used in the treatment of a frequent illness but may not have been developed for another, more rare indication.
